Red Custard Apple

Annona reticulata

“Sweet & cream raspberry cheesecake” — dense, perfumed, and almost dessert by itself.

Red custard apple is a tropical Annona with a soft, custard‑like interior and a reticulated (net‑patterned) skin. It’s a good example of how fruit texture, aroma, and seed arrangement can all be used as ID clues, not just leaf shape.

Botanical Details Family, origin, tree form
  • Family: Annonaceae (same family as cherimoya and soursop).
  • Origin: Central America and the Caribbean, now grown in many tropical regions.
  • Tree form: Small to medium tree with a rounded, somewhat open canopy.
  • Leaves: Simple, entire, often drooping; aromatic when crushed.
  • Fruit: Heart‑shaped to rounded, with a net‑like skin pattern that can blush red or pink.
Identification Tips What to look for
  • Look for the reticulated skin and slightly irregular heart shape.
  • When ripe, the fruit yields slightly to pressure and has a strong, sweet aroma.
  • The interior is creamy and segmented, with dark seeds embedded in the pulp.
  • Leaves are long, narrow, and drooping, often clustered near branch tips.
Flavor & Texture Notes Tasting profile
  • Texture is thick, custard‑like, and spoonable.
  • Flavor leans toward raspberry, vanilla, and condensed milk.
  • Best eaten fully soft; underripe fruit can be starchy and bland.

Black Sapote

Diospyros digyna

“Chocolate pudding fruit” — looks like dessert, tastes like mild cocoa and dates.

Black sapote is a persimmon relative that only reveals its character when fully ripe. It’s a good reminder that color change and softness are critical ripeness indicators, not just calendar date or size.

Botanical Details Family, habit, leaves
  • Family: Ebenaceae (persimmon family).
  • Origin: Mexico and Central America; now grown in warm subtropical climates.
  • Tree: Evergreen, medium‑sized, with dense, glossy foliage.
  • Leaves: Simple, entire, dark green, with a leathery feel.
  • Fruit: Green when immature, turning olive to dark brown as it ripens.
Identification & Ripeness Visual cues
  • Unripe fruit is firm and green; do not eat at this stage.
  • Ripe fruit becomes soft, with slightly wrinkled skin and dark flesh.
  • Cut fruit reveals a smooth, pudding‑like interior with few seeds.
Flavor & Use How people eat it
  • Flavor is mild cocoa, dates, and caramel rather than intense chocolate.
  • Often mixed with milk, vanilla, or sweeteners to make desserts.
  • Texture makes it ideal for smoothies and chilled puddings.

Star Apple / Caimito

Chrysophyllum cainito

“Condensed milk fruit” — creamy, sweet, and stunning when sliced across the star.

Star apple gets its name from the star pattern formed by the seeds when you cut it across the equator. It’s a good exercise in noticing latex, leaf undersides, and cut‑surface patterns as ID features.

Botanical Details Family, latex, leaves
  • Family: Sapotaceae (many latex‑bearing trees).
  • Origin: Caribbean and Central America.
  • Latex: Present in skin and leaves; sticky when cut.
  • Leaves: Dark green above, with a distinctive metallic bronze underside.
  • Fruit: Round, purple or green, with a star pattern inside.
Identification Tips Field clues
  • Flip a leaf: the bronze underside is a strong ID marker.
  • Cut the fruit across the middle to reveal the star‑shaped seed pattern.
  • Latex can make the skin sticky; fruit is usually scooped, not bitten into.
Flavor & Texture Tasting notes
  • Texture is jelly‑like near the center, firmer near the rind.
  • Flavor is sweet, creamy, and reminiscent of condensed milk.
  • Best eaten chilled, with the rind and latex avoided.

Roxburgh Fig

Ficus auriculata

“Mildly sweet, intense berry aroma, raspberry taste without tartness.”

Roxburgh fig is a cauliflorous fig — meaning the fruit emerges directly from the trunk and large branches. It’s a perfect example of how fruit position on the tree can be a major identification clue.

Botanical Details Family, habit, fruiting
  • Family: Moraceae (fig and mulberry family).
  • Origin: India and Southeast Asia.
  • Leaves: Very large, rough‑textured, often with an “ear‑like” shape.
  • Fruiting habit: Cauliflorous — fruit clusters appear on trunk and main limbs.
Identification & Habitat Where and how it grows
  • Look for large leaves and fruit attached directly to the trunk.
  • Often found in humid, forested areas or near water.
  • Like other figs, it has a specialized pollination relationship with fig wasps.
Flavor Profile Tasting notes
  • Flavor is mild but with a strong berry aroma.
  • Less tart than raspberries, more gentle and floral.
  • Texture is soft, with small seeds similar to other figs.

Cecropia peltata

Cecropia peltata

“Gummy worm fruit” — playful texture, fig‑and‑kiwi flavor, and a wild architecture.

Cecropia is a fast‑growing pioneer tree with a hollow, segmented trunk often inhabited by ants. Its fruit looks like soft, dangling spikes that you can strip and eat like candy. It’s a great example of mutualism (tree + ants) and unusual fruit form.

Botanical Details Family, structure, mutualism
  • Family: Urticaceae.
  • Origin: Tropical Americas; common in disturbed sites and forest edges.
  • Trunk: Hollow internodes often occupied by ants that defend the tree.
  • Leaves: Large, palmately lobed, giving a very distinctive silhouette.
  • Fruit: Long, soft, finger‑like structures that can be peeled and eaten.
Identification & Ecology Field and ecosystem notes
  • Look for umbrella‑like leaves and a very open, light‑loving crown.
  • Often one of the first trees to colonize cleared or disturbed land.
  • Ants living in the trunk defend the tree from herbivores — a classic mutualism.
Flavor & Texture Tasting notes
  • Flavor is a mix of fig, kiwi, and mild tropical sweetness.
  • Texture is soft and chewy, like natural gummy candy.
  • Usually eaten fresh, by stripping the outer layer and eating the inner pulp.
